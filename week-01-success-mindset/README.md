# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

## Answer

I believe that a boring, repeatable process done consistently over time beats talent, speed, and shortcuts almost every time. Most people around me disagree because it looks slow and unglamorous. They want visible results now, and they quit the process right before it would have paid off. I have come to trust the opposite: that the quiet work you do when no one is watching, repeated long enough, compounds into results that no burst of talent or last-minute effort can match. Time is what separates the people who talk about outcomes from the people who actually reach them.

# Assignment 2. What are the top 3 objective truths you discovered through experimentation and results?

---

## Truth #1

### Truth

Time reveals everything, the value of your work, the character of people, and whether a decision was right, but only if you stay in long enough to see it.

### Evidence from my life

I have watched efforts that felt invisible for months suddenly start to matter once enough time had passed. Skills I practised with no immediate reward, and public writing I published with no audience at first, only showed their value later, once I had kept going long enough for the results to surface. Every time I was tempted to judge something too early, waiting proved me wrong.

---

## Truth #2

### Truth

You suffer far less when you stop spending energy on things outside your control and pour it into the few things you can actually influence.

### Evidence from my life

When I used to worry about outcomes I could not control, like other people's opinions, timing, or luck, it drained me and changed nothing. Once I shifted my focus to the inputs I owned (showing up, doing the work, documenting it), the stress dropped and the results improved. The situations did not change; my energy did, and that made all the difference.

---

## Truth #3

### Truth

The process matters, not just the end product, because how you build something determines whether it lasts and whether you can repeat it.

### Evidence from my life

Whenever I chased only the finished result and skipped the process, the outcome was fragile and I could not reproduce it. When I respected the process, doing the fundamentals properly, documenting each step publicly, and building the habit instead of the one-off win, the results held up and I could do it again. My DevOps journey has made this obvious: the engineers who understand *how* something works, not just *that* it works, are the ones who can be trusted with real systems.

## Answer

**Yes.**

**What emotion did you feel?**

The feeling that stayed with me was not guilt about being caught. It was the quiet knowledge that I had cheated the process. When I took a shortcut, even one that "worked," something felt hollow about the result. I could not fully own it, because I knew it had not been earned the right way, and I could not confidently repeat it. ## Step 2: Your 5-Month Routine + Focus Blocks

### Weekly Routine

#### My Weekly Routine

* **Monday to Friday (after 11:00 PM):** 3 hours of focused DMI / DevOps deep work, a quiet, distraction-free window once the day is done
* **Saturday morning:** Longer DMI session (8 hours) to complete the week's assignment and hands-on labs while I'm fresh
* **Saturday or Sunday:** Weekly review, publish my public write-up (Hashnode / Medium), and plan the coming week
* **Daily:** Short reading block from my book list before bed

---

### Focus Blocks

#### When Will You Do DMI Work? (Days + Time)

Weekday nights after 11:00 PM for consistent daily progress (my quietest, most distraction-free window), plus a longer Saturday morning block for the main assignment and hands-on labs.

#### How Many Sessions Per Week?

6 sessions per week: five short weekday-night sessions and one longer Saturday-morning session.

---

### Distraction Rules

#### My Distraction Rules

My single biggest distraction is not my phone. It is the **temptation to be complacent**: to coast, to tell myself I have done "enough," to skip a session because I am already ahead. My rules to fight it:

* **No zero days:** every day gets at least one small, documented action, even on low-energy days.
* **Show up before I feel like it:** I start the session first and decide how I feel afterward, not before.
* **Publish to stay accountable:** documenting in public each week makes complacency visible, so I cannot quietly drift.
* **Phone out of reach** during deep-work blocks, and social media only after the day's session is done.

# Reflection - Week 1

### Biggest insight I got about myself this week

My biggest strength and my biggest risk are the same thing: I trust the long game. That patience keeps me consistent, but it can also let me rationalise slowing down. Naming that pattern is the first time I have seen it clearly enough to manage it.

### My biggest weakness/loop I noticed

The temptation to be complacent once I feel "ahead." I can do the hard work, but I sometimes reward myself by easing off exactly when momentum matters most, turning progress into a reason to coast.

### One system I will implement from this week (exact habit + time)

**A "no zero days" rule:** every weeknight at 11:00 PM I do at least one small, documented DevOps action, even 15 minutes, and log it. The bar is showing up, not doing a lot, so complacency never gets a foothold.
